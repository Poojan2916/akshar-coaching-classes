💡 Exam Tips — Chapter 2
🎯 Akbar vs Aurangzeb is a classic exam topic. Remember: Akbar = Inclusive (abolished jizya, married Rajputs, sulh-i kul). Aurangzeb = Exclusive (reimposed jizya, banned music, destroyed temples).
🎯 Qutub Minar — built by Qutub-ud-din Aibak, materials from 27 temples. Name "Quwwat-ul-Islam" = "Might of Islam."
🎯 "Musical pillars" = Vitthala temple in Hampi (Vijayanagara). The monolithic pillars produce musical notes when struck.
📌 The Baburnama is important — it is Babur's AUTOBIOGRAPHY. The Razmnama is the Persian translation of the Mahabharata (NOT Babur's book).
📌 Vijayanagara = founded by Harihara and Bukka (1336); Hampi is capital; destroyed at Battle of Talikota (1565). The kingdom was a "center of art and trade" and attracted Portuguese traders.
📝 Short Answer Practice — Ch 2

1. What was the sulh-i kul policy of Akbar? Why was it important?

Sulh-i kul = "peace with all." Akbar introduced it to promote tolerance of all religions in his diverse empire. He abolished jizya (tax on non-Muslims), welcomed Rajput leaders into his court, and encouraged interfaith dialogue. It stabilized his empire by winning the support of diverse communities.

2. Why did Muhammad bin Tughlaq shift his capital to Daulatabad?

He believed Daulatabad's central location would allow better control over his expanding empire and reduce the threat of Mongol invasions in Delhi. However, the forced migration caused immense suffering to people and the plan failed. He was forced to shift the capital back to Delhi.

3. How did the Mughals use marriage alliances? Give a specific example.

Akbar used marriage alliances with Rajput princesses to politically integrate regional leaders into his court. By marrying into Rajput families, he secured the loyalty of powerful local dynasties and reduced resistance. This allowed Rajput chiefs to serve as generals in his army, strengthening Mughal power.

🏰 Key Facts — Maratha Empire
📌 Raigad Fort = Primary symbol of Maratha sovereignty. Located in Sahyadri mountains. Site of Shivaji's 1674 coronation.
📌 Forts = "Core of the state" — administrative centers, storehouses, military bases. Hard to conquer from mountains.
📌 Adnyapatra (1715) by Ramachandrapant Amatya — warns against Portuguese, English, Dutch, French ("hat-wearers"). Says their trade should be controlled, don't allow fortified buildings.
📌 Guerrilla warfare — mobility, surprise attacks, using rugged terrain of Sahyadri mountains. Marathas' biggest military advantage.

📝 Short Answer Practice — Marathas

1. Why were forts considered the "core of the state" for the Marathas?

Forts were the center of Maratha military, administrative, and economic life. They were strategically built on hilltops, making them nearly impossible to conquer. They served as storehouses for food and weapons, allowing long campaigns. Forts also allowed the Marathas to retreat and regroup after battles against larger armies, effectively neutralizing the Mughals' numerical advantage.

2. What was the significance of the Adnyapatra? Who wrote it and when?

The Adnyapatra was written by Ramachandrapant Amatya in 1715. It is a political document providing advice on governance and defense. It warned against the European "hat-wearers" (Portuguese, English, Dutch, French), identifying them as more than traders — they had territorial and religious ambitions. The document advised strictly controlling European trade and forbidding them from building fortified buildings. It shows the Marathas had early, clear strategic thinking about colonialism.
