Section B: Short Answer Questions (15 Questions – 3 Points Each)
1. State the function of the cell wall in plant cells.
? The cell wall is a rigid outer layer composed of cellulose that provides vital structural support and
protection to the plant cell.
? It helps the plant maintain its shape even when subjected to high wind speeds or varying
C
atmospheric pressure.
? This structure is porous in nature and allows the free movement of water and dissolved substances
into and out of the cell.
2. What is the role of Lactobacillus in curd formation?
? LactobAacillus is a beneficial bacterium that promotes the formation of curd by converting milk into a
thicker consistency.
? It functions by feeding on the lactose sugar present in milk and converting it into lactic acid through
fermentation.
? This chemical change lowers the pH of the milk, causing the proteins to coagulate and set the milk
into curd.
3. How does yeast help in the baking industry?
? Yeast is added to dough where it performs anaerobic respiration to break down sugar into alcohol
and gas.
? The carbon dioxide gas produced during this process forms bubbles that get trapped, causing the
dough to rise significantly.
? This biological reaction ensures that final products like bread and cakes remain soft, porous, and
fluffy for consumption.
4. Define pathogens and explain how they spread.
? Pathogens are harmful microorganisms like bacteria or viruses that are responsible for causing
diseases in plants and animals.
? They can spread from an infected person to a healthy one through contaminated air, water, food, or
direct physical contact.
? Some pathogens are also carried by insects like mosquitoes or houseflies, which act as carriers of
the disease-causing microbes.
5. Why are vacuoles larger in plant cells than in animal cells?
? Plant cells contain a single large central vacuole that is needed to store cell sap, water, and essential
minerals for the plant.

4
? This large size is necessary to exert turgor pressure against the cell wall, which keeps the plant cell
rigid and upright.
? In contrast, animal cells only require smaller, temporary vacuoles as they do not rely on internal
water pressure for structural support.
6. Explain the function of Mitochondria.
? Mitochondria are double-membraned organelles that are responsible for the process of cellular
respiration to produce energy.
? They oxidize food molecules in the presence oCf oxygen to release energy in the form of ATP for the
cell.
? Because they provide the energy required for all cellular activities, they are commonly referred to as
the powerhouse of the cell.
7. What are the three types of plastids and their functions?
? Chloroplasts are green-coloreCd plastids containing chlorophyll that are essential for the process of
photosynthesis in plants.
? Chromoplasts are colored plastids that provide distinct red, orange, or yellow colors to various
flowers and fruits to attract pollinators.
? Leucoplasts are colorless plastids that serve as storage units for food materials like starch, proteins,
A
and oils in roots.
8. Describe the shape and movement of Amoeba.
? Amoeba is a unicellular organism that does not have a fixed shape and constantly changes its body
contours as it moves.
? It moves by extending finger-like projections called pseudopodia or "false feet" in the direction of its
intended movement.
? These pseudopodia also help the organism in capturing food particles by surrounding them and
forming a temporary food vacuole.
9. How do microorganisms increase soil fertility?
? Certain bacteria and blue-green algae have the ability to fix atmospheric nitrogen and convert it into
usable nitrates.
? These nitrogenous compounds are released into the soil, making it richer in nutrients required for
healthy plant growth.
? This natural enrichment process reduces the need for farmers to use expensive and harmful
chemical fertilizers on their crops.
10. What is fermentation?
? Fermentation is a biological process in which microorganisms like yeast break down sugar into
alcohol and carbon dioxide.
? This process occurs in the absence of oxygen and is widely used in the production of vinegar, wine,
and bread.
? It was first discovered by Louis Pasteur and remains a fundamental technique in the modern food
and beverage industry.
11. State the importance of the Plasma Membrane.

5
? The plasma membrane is a thin, flexible, and selectively permeable layer that surrounds the entire
cytoplasm of the cell.
? It controls the entry and exit of specific substances, ensuring that the internal environment of the
cell remains stable.
? This membrane also provides a degree of physical protection and separates the cell contents from
the external surroundings.
12. Briefly explain the role of decomposers.
? Decomposers are microorganisms like bacterCia and fungi that break down dead plant and animal
remains into simpler substances.
? By performing this task, they clean the environment and prevent the accumulation of waste and
decaying matter in nature.
? The nutrients released during decomposition are returned to the soil, where they can be reused by
living plants for growth. C
13. What is the difference between unicellular and multicellular organisms?
? Unicellular organisms consist of a single cell that performs all the necessary life functions like
digestion and reproduction.
? Multicellular organisms are made up of millions of cells that are specialized to perform specific
A
tasks within the body structure.
? Examples of unicellular organisms include Amoeba and Bacteria, while plants, animals, and humans
are examples of multicellular organisms.
14. Mention two uses of algae in everyday life.
? Certain types of algae like Spirulina are used as a highly nutritious food supplement due to their
high protein and vitamin content.
? Algae are also used in the manufacturing of various commercial products such as ice creams, jellies,
and even certain medicines.
15. Why are viruses considered to be on the borderline of life?
? Viruses do not show any signs of life or metabolic activity as long as they are outside a living host
cell.
? However, once they enter a host organism, they utilize the host's machinery to multiply and
reproduce like living things.
? Because they exhibit characteristics of both states depending on their environment, they are placed
on the borderline of life.

1. Compare Plant and Animal cells based on four structural features.
? Plant cells possess a thick, rigid cell wall made of cellulose for support, while animal cells
completely lack a cell wall structure.
? Large central vacuoles are present in plant cells to maintain turgidity, whereas animal cells have
several small and temporary vacuoles.
? Chloroplasts and other plastids are found in plant cells for food production, but these organelles are
never found in animal cells.
? The nucleus is generally pushed to the periphery in plant cells due to the large vacuole, while it is
centrally located in animal cells.
2. Explain the process of Nitrogen Fixation by Rhizobium.
? Rhizobium bacteria live in a symbiotic relationship within the root nodules of leguminous plants
like peas and beans.
? These bacteria take atmospheric nitrogen, which plants cannot use directly, and convert it into
water-soluble nitrates.

7
? The host plant absorbs these nitrates from the soil and uses them to synthesize essential proteins
and other compounds.
? In return, the plant provides the bacteria with food and shelter, completing a mutually beneficial
cycle that enriches the soil.
3. Describe the four main groups of microorganisms with examples.
? Bacteria are unicellular organisms that can be rod-shaped (Bacillus) or spherical (Coccus) and are
found in almost every environment.
? Fungi are a group of organisms that include uCnicellular yeast and multicellular molds, often growing
on decaying organic matter.
? Protozoa are unicellular animal-like organisms that are mostly found in aquatic environments and
include organisms like Amoeba.
? Algae are simple plant-like organisms that contain chlorophyll and can be unicellular like
Chlamydomonas or multicellCular like Spirogyra.
4. What are the functions of the Cytoplasm?
? Cytoplasm is a jelly-like substance that fills the space between the cell membrane and the nucleus,
holding all the cell organelles.
? It acts as a medium for various chemical reactions and metabolic processes that are absolutely
A
essential for the cell's survival.
? The cytoplasm facilitates the movement of materials and nutrients from one part of the cell to
another through intracellular streaming.
? It also provides the necessary shape to the cell and serves as a site for the storage of vital chemicals
like salts and sugars.
5. Explain the role of microorganisms in medicine.
? Microorganisms are used to produce antibiotics like Penicillin, which are used to kill or stop the
growth of disease-causing bacteria.
? They are also essential in the production of vaccines, which help the body develop immunity against
specific viruses and bacteria.
? Certain bacteria are used to produce vitamins and even human insulin, which is life-saving for
people suffering from diabetes.
? Additionally, microbes are used in research to understand the genetic basis of diseases and develop
new treatment methods for illnesses.
6. Describe the structure and function of Plastids.
? Plastids are double-membraned organelles found only in plant cells and are classified into three
types based on their specific colors.
? Chloroplasts contain green chlorophyll and are responsible for capturing sunlight to perform the
vital process of photosynthesis.
? Chromoplasts contain various pigments that give attractive colors to flowers and fruits to aid in
pollination and seed dispersal.
? Leucoplasts are colorless and are specialized for the storage of nutrients such as starch, proteins,
and fats in the plant body.

7. How do microorganisms contribute to environmental cleaning?
? Saprophytic bacteria and fungi act as decomposers that break down organic waste into simpler,
harmless inorganic substances for the soil.
? This process prevents the buildup of dead plants and animal remains, effectively cleaning the
surface of the Earth naturally.
? During sewage treatment, anaerobic bacteria are used to break down human waste and produce

? By recycling nutrients back into the soil, theseC microorganisms ensure the continued health and

8. Explain the levels of organization in living organisms.
? The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of life, where all the fundamental processes of an
organism occur at a microscopic level.
? A group of similar cells perfoCrming a specific function forms a tissue, such as muscle tissue in
animals or vascular tissue in plants.
? Different tissues work together to form an organ, like the heart or a leaf, to perform a more complex
biological task.
? Multiple organs coordinate their activities to form an organ system, which eventually constitutes the
A
complete living organism structure.
9. What are the harmful effects of microorganisms?
? Many microorganisms act as pathogens that cause serious diseases such as cholera, malaria, and
tuberculosis in human populations.
? Certain fungi and bacteria cause spoilage of food items, making them unfit for consumption and
leading to cases of food poisoning.
? Microbes are also responsible for causing various diseases in crops and livestock, which can lead to
significant economic losses.
? They can also cause damage to non-living materials like leather, wood, and paper if they are kept in
moist or unventilated conditions.
10. Describe the structure of a typical bacterial cell.
? A bacterial cell is a prokaryotic cell that lacks a well-defined nucleus and membrane-bound
organelles like mitochondria or plastids.
? It is surrounded by a cell wall and a plasma membrane that protect the internal genetic material and
the jelly-like cytoplasm.
? The genetic material is present as a single circular chromosome located in a region of the cytoplasm
called the nucleoid area.
? Many bacteria also possess small hair-like structures called pili or a long whip-like flagellum that
assists them in their movement.

? The nucleus is a large, spherical organelle that serves as the primary control center for all cellular
activities and metabolic processes.
? It is enclosed by a double-layered nuclear membrane that contains numerous pores to regulate the
flow of materials to the cytoplasm.
? Inside the nucleus, a jelly-like substance called nucleoplasm contains the nucleolus and a network of
thread-like chromatin.
C
? During cell division, the chromatin condenses into distinct chromosomes, which carry genes made

? The nucleus is responsible for storing the genetic information of the organism and passing it from
one generation to the next.
? It also coordinates cell growth, reproduction, and protein synthesis, making it the most vital part of
C
the eukaryotic cell structure.
2. Compare the characteristics of the five major groups of microorganisms.
? Bacteria are microscopic unicellular organisms that exist in various shapes and can be found in
every possible environment on Earth.
? Fungi include both unicellular yeast and multicellular molds that obtain their nutrition by absorbing
A
organic matter from decaying sources.
? Protozoa are unicellular animal-like organisms that move using cilia, flagella, or pseudopodia and
are often found in water bodies.
? Algae are simple plant-like organisms that contain chlorophyll for photosynthesis and can exist as
single cells or large multicellular colonies.
? Viruses are unique entities that show signs of life only within a host cell and are much smaller than
all other microorganisms.
? Each group plays a distinct role in the ecosystem, ranging from disease-causing pathogens to
essential decomposers and oxygen producers.
3. Explain the Nitrogen Cycle and its importance in nature.
? The nitrogen cycle is the continuous process by which nitrogen moves from the atmosphere into the
soil, living things, and back.
? It begins with nitrogen fixation by bacteria like Rhizobium or through the action of lightning,
converting nitrogen gas into nitrates.
? Plants absorb these nitrates to build proteins, which are then consumed by animals to meet their
own nutritional requirements.
? When plants and animals die, decomposers convert the nitrogenous waste back into ammonia and
other simple compounds in the soil.
? Denitrifying bacteria then convert some of these nitrates back into nitrogen gas, which is released
into the atmosphere to maintain balance.
? This cycle is crucial because nitrogen is an essential component of proteins, chlorophyll, nucleic
acids, and vitamins in all living beings.

? While many microbes are used to produce food, others must be controlled through preservation

5. Differentiate between Prokaryotic and Eukaryotic cells with examples.
? Prokaryotic cells are generally smaller and simpler, lacking a well-defined nucleus and
membrane-bound organelles like mitochondria.
? Eukaryotic cells are larger and more complex, possessing a distinct nucleus surrounded by a nuclear
A
membrane and various organelles.
? In prokaryotes, the genetic material is found directly in the cytoplasm as a nucleoid, while in

? Prokaryotic cells are found in unicellular organisms like bacteria and blue-green algae, which

? Eukaryotic cells make up all multicellular organisms, including plants, fungi, and animals, as well as

? The presence of membrane-bound compartments in eukaryotes allows for more specialized and
efficient functioning compared to the simpler prokaryotic structure.
