VI. Case Studies (1 Mark Each - 4 Questions per Case)

Case Study 1: The Force of Gravity

Gravity is a force of attraction that acts between all objects with mass. On Earth, we see this
force in action when a ball falls to the ground or water flows down from a tap. Gravity is not
unique to Earth; it is a universal force that keeps planets in orbit and holds the atmosphere in
place. As an object moves farther away from the center of the Earth, the gravitational pull on it
becomes weaker. This is why astronauts in orbiting spacecraft experience a state of free fall and

feel weightless, as there are no contact forces pushing against them.

Case Study 3: The Electroscope and Charges

When you drop an object into water, it experiences an upward push known as buoyant force or
upthrust. According to Archimedes' principle, this force is equal to the weight of the water
displaced by the object. A piece of wood floats because its weight is less than the buoyant force,
while a metal coin sinks because its weight is greater. Density also plays a role; materials less
dense than water tend to float. Buoyant force also exists in gases, which is why helium-filled
balloons rise into the sky.

An electroscope is a device used to detect the presence of electric charges. It consists of a metal
rod with two thin metal leaves. When a charged object is brought into contact with the rod, the
charge travels to the leaves. Since like charges repel, the leaves spread apart. Static electricity is
the accumulation of electric charge on the surface of an object, usually through friction. Unlike
gravity, which only attracts, electrostatic forces can attract or repel objects depending on
whether the charges are opposite or similar.
