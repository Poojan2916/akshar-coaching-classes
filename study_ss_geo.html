🌿 What are Natural Resources?
🔑 Natural Resources = gifts of nature used by humans. Include land, water, forests, wildlife, minerals, soil, air.
Resources are classified in multiple ways based on: exhaustibility, origin, ownership, and development status.
Classification A: Exhaustibility
Renewable ResourcesCan be replenished naturally. Examples: Water (in water cycle), Forests (regrow), Solar energy, Wind energy, Tidal energy.
Non-renewable ResourcesFinite, take millions of years to form. Examples: Coal, Petroleum, Natural gas, Minerals like iron ore, bauxite.
Classification B: Origin
BioticLiving sources: Forests, animals, fisheries, crops
AbioticNon-living sources: Land, water, air, minerals, metals
Classification C: Development Status
Potential ResourcesExist but not yet exploited (e.g., uranium in Rajasthan)
Actual ResourcesAlready surveyed and being used (e.g., coal mined in West Bengal)
Reserve ResourcesActual resources kept for future use
Stock ResourcesResources whose full potential is not yet determined
🌳 Forest & Wildlife Resources
ConceptDetails
Deforestation causesAgriculture expansion, urbanization, logging, fuel wood collection, mining activities
Effects of deforestationSoil erosion, floods, drought, loss of biodiversity, climate change, displacement of tribal communities
Chipko Movement1973, Uttarakhand; villagers (especially women led by Gaura Devi) hugged trees to prevent logging. Means "to cling."
Project Tiger1973 — to protect tiger populations. Created tiger reserves across India.
Wildlife Protection Act1972 — comprehensive law protecting wild animals and plants. Creates protected areas.
Biosphere ReserveLarge protected areas with core zone (no human activity) + buffer zone + transition zone.
National ParkHighest protection — no human activity allowed. Examples: Kaziranga, Jim Corbett, Gir.
Wildlife SanctuaryProtection of specific species — some human activity allowed.
Social ForestryCommunity-based planting — people grow trees for their own use
AgroforestryTrees + crops grown together — improves soil, microclimate, and income
🎯 Chipko year = 1973, NOT 1972. Wildlife Protection Act = 1972. Project Tiger = 1973. Don't confuse them!
⛏️ Mineral Resources
Minerals Classification
Metallic MineralsIron ore, Bauxite (aluminium), Copper, Gold, Manganese, Zinc
Non-Metallic MineralsCoal, Petroleum, Limestone, Mica, Rock salt, Gypsum
Ferrous MineralsContain iron — Iron ore, Manganese, Chromite, Nickel
Non-Ferrous MineralsNo iron — Copper, Bauxite, Gold, Lead, Zinc
⚙️ Iron Ore
Magnetite (best quality, 72% iron), Hematite (most used, 50-60% iron). Found in: Odisha, Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh.
🪵 Coal
Anthracite (best quality) → Bituminous → Lignite → Peat. India = 4th largest coal reserve.
🛢️ Petroleum
"Black gold". Refined to get petrol, diesel, kerosene, LPG. Found offshore in Mumbai High.
⚠️ Over-exploitation of minerals = Non-renewable. Once gone, cannot be replaced. Conservation is essential.

💡 Exam Tips — Natural Resources
🎯 Chipko = 1973, Wildlife Act = 1972, Project Tiger = 1973. These 3 dates are commonly tested together.
🎯 Petroleum = "Black gold" (because it's as valuable as gold and is black-brown crude oil). Natural gas = "blue fuel". LPG = Liquefied Petroleum Gas.
🎯 Magnetite = highest quality iron ore (72% pure iron). Hematite = most widely used. Remember: "Magnet = Most pure."
📌 Renewable ≠ unlimited. Water, forests, and fish stocks CAN be depleted if overused beyond their natural replenishment rate.
📌 India's major minerals by region: Coal (Jharkhand, Odisha) | Petroleum (Mumbai High, Gujarat) | Copper (Rajasthan) | Bauxite (Odisha, Jharkhand) | Iron ore (Jharkhand, Odisha, Karnataka).
🏔️ Land Resources
🔑 Land is the most important natural resource — supports life, agriculture, industry, habitation.
India's land use is classified into: Forest land, Non-agricultural land, Agricultural land, Cultivable wasteland, Current fallows, Other fallows, Net sown area.
Land Use CategoryMeaning
Net Sown AreaLand actually cultivated (one crop per year)
Gross Cropped AreaNet sown area + area sown more than once (double/triple cropped)
Current FallowLand left uncultivated for one year for soil fertility restoration
Cultivable WastelandLand that could be cultivated but isn't — due to neglect or poor conditions
WastelandBarren, rocky, desert land — uncultivable
Land DegradationDeterioration of land quality due to erosion, waterlogging, salinity, or overgrazing

Soil Erosion & Conservation
Type of ErosionCauseSolution
Sheet ErosionRainwater flows uniformly over the field removing topsoilCover crops, mulching
Rill ErosionSmall rills/channels form due to rainfall runoffCheck dams, contour ploughing
Gully ErosionDeep gullies cut into land (creates "ravines"). Common in UP, MP, Bihar.Plugging gullies, afforestation
Wind ErosionWind blows away dry, loose topsoil. Common in Rajasthan.Shelter belts (trees planted in rows)
📌 Gully erosion creates "Bad Land" or "Ravines" — unfit for cultivation. Parts of Chambal basin in MP and UP suffer from this.

💧 Water Resources
🔑 Water = renewable resource (through water cycle) but freshwater is only 2.5% of total water, of which only 1% is accessible for human use.
ConceptDetails
Water CycleEvaporation → Condensation → Precipitation → Collection → Evaporation again
Sources of freshwaterRivers (glaciers), Groundwater, Lakes, Rainfall
Water scarcityWhen demand exceeds supply. India = water-stressed country in some regions.
Irrigation methodsCanal irrigation (Punjab, Haryana), Drip irrigation (Karnataka), Sprinkler, Tanks (South India)
Rainwater HarvestingCollecting rainwater for storage and reuse. Rooftop RWH, Kund, Johad, Check dams.
Groundwater depletionOver-extraction of groundwater through deep bore wells faster than recharge rate

🎯 Iron ore: Magnetite = 72% iron. Hematite = 50-60% iron. India = 4th largest coal reserve. India has 22-25% of world's iron ore reserves.

📋 Written Practice — Short Answers

1. Distinguish between renewable and non-renewable resources with two examples each.

Renewable resources can be replenished naturally within a short time period. Examples: Solar energy, Wind energy, Forests (when managed properly).

Non-renewable resources are finite and take millions of years to form. Once used, they cannot be replaced within a human time scale. Examples: Coal (takes millions of years), Petroleum (cannot be replenished), Iron ore (limited deposits).

2. What is the Chipko Movement? What was its significance?

The Chipko Movement began in 1973 in the Uttarakhand hills. Women, led by Gaura Devi, hugged trees to physically prevent logging contractors from cutting forests. "Chipko" means to cling/hug.

Significance: (i) It saved forests from commercial felling. (ii) It highlighted the link between forests and community survival — especially for women who depend on forests for fuel, fodder, and water. (iii) It inspired similar movements across India. (iv) It became a model of non-violent environmental activism worldwide.
