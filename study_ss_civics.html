🗳️ What is Universal Adult Franchise?
🔑 Universal Adult Franchise = The right of every adult citizen (18+) to vote irrespective of caste, religion, gender, education, or wealth.

Principle: One person = One vote = One value
Constitutional basis: Article 326 of the Indian Constitution

📜 Key Constitutional Articles
Article 326
Mandates Universal Adult Franchise — every election shall be based on adult suffrage (18+). This is the constitutional backbone of Indian democracy.
Article 324
Establishes the Election Commission of India as an independent constitutional body for superintendence, direction, and control of elections.
61st Constitutional Amendment (1988)
Lowered voting age from 21 to 18 years — included youth in political participation.

💡 Exam Tips — Franchise
🎯 61st Amendment = 1988 = voting age lowered to 18. This is a very common MCQ. Don't confuse with 42nd Amendment (which changed other things).
🎯 ECI = Article 324 (not 326). Article 326 = Universal Adult Franchise. Learn both separately!
🎯 Rajya Sabha = "Upper House" = represents States = term 6 years = Vice President presides. Lok Sabha = "Lower House" = represents People = term 5 years = Speaker presides.
📌 FPTP = "First Past The Post" — the candidate with the MOST votes wins, even if they don't have 50%+. Simple, gives stable majority.

⚖️ Structure of Indian Judiciary
🔑 India has a Single Integrated Judiciary — decisions of higher courts are binding on all lower courts. Supreme Court at apex.
Hierarchy: Supreme Court → High Court → District Court → Subordinate Courts
CourtLocationKey Facts
Supreme CourtNew DelhiChief Justice + max 33 other judges. Retirement age: 65. Appointed by President.
High CourtState capital (Benches in other cities)Oldest = Calcutta High Court. Retirement age: 62.
District CourtDistrict headquartersCivil cases = District Judge; Criminal cases = Sessions Judge. Appointed by Governor.
Lok AdalatAcross IndiaFirst held 1982 in Junagarh, Gujarat. Quick, inexpensive, compromise-based. Decisions final, no appeal.

🔑 PIL — Public Interest Litigation
PIL = Public Interest Litigation

• Any citizen or organization can file it — even without being a direct victim
• Can be a simple letter or telegram to High Court or Supreme Court
• Court can also take Suo Motu action (on its own, based on news reports)
• Has addressed: pollution, bonded labour, custodial torture, human rights
• Called the "poor man's court" — very low cost, highly accessible

💡 Exam Tips — Judiciary
🎯 Remember: SC = 65 retirement age. HC = 62 retirement age. District judges appointed by Governor (not President).
🎯 Oldest High Court = Calcutta High Court. First Lok Adalat = 1982 in Junagarh, Gujarat. First woman SC judge = Fathima Beevi.
🎯 Lok Adalat = NO court fees + decisions are FINAL (no appeal possible). This is commonly tested.
📌 PIL can be filed even through a letter or telegram to the Supreme Court or High Court. The court can also take action Suo Motu (on its own). This is what makes it unique and accessible to the poor.
📌 Original Jurisdiction of SC = disputes BETWEEN states (e.g., water disputes). Goes directly to SC without passing through High Court.
📝 Short Answer Practice — Judiciary

1. What is Public Interest Litigation (PIL)? Why is it called the "poor man's court"?

PIL (Public Interest Litigation) allows any citizen or organization to file a case on behalf of those whose rights are being violated, even without being a direct victim. It can be initiated through a simple letter or telegram (no complex procedures, very low cost). The court can also take Suo Motu action. It is called the "poor man's court" because: no high court fees are required, complex legal formalities are waived, and it brings justice to those who cannot afford high-profile lawyers.

2. Explain any three measures that ensure the independence of the Indian Judiciary.

(i) Security of Tenure: SC judges serve until 65, HC judges until 62 — they cannot be removed easily or at will.
(ii) Fixed Salaries: Salaries are charged to the Consolidated Fund of India and cannot be reduced — prevents financial pressure.
(iii) Impeachment: Removal requires a difficult process — address passed by both Houses of Parliament with special majority, then Presidential order. This prevents hasty dismissal for political reasons.
