Section B — Short Answer Questions

Short Answer 1

What was sulh-i-kul? How did it help Akbar rule his empire?

Sulh-i-kul = 'peace with all.' Akbar promoted religious tolerance — abolished jizya on non-Muslims, married Rajput princesses, welcomed scholars of all faiths at his court, and created a diverse administration. This policy helped unify his vast, multi-religious empire, won loyalty from Rajput leaders and common people alike, and reduced internal conflict.
Short Answer 2

Why were Maratha forts considered the 'core of the state'?

Maratha forts built on high hilltops were centers of governance, military operations, and supply storage. They were nearly impossible to conquer due to terrain. After any defeat in open battle, the Marathas could retreat to forts, regroup, and resume guerrilla warfare. Forts gave them resilience and strategic depth against the numerically superior Mughal army.
Short Answer 3

Explain the significance of the Adnyapatra.

Written by Ramachandrapant Amatya in 1715, the Adnyapatra provided governance advice and warned against 'hat-wearers' (European traders — Portuguese, English, Dutch, French). It recognized that European traders were not just traders but had territorial and religious ambitions. It advised strict control of their trade and forbade them from building fortified structures on Maratha territory.
Short Answer 4

Who was Rani Durgavati? What was her significance?

Rani Durgavati was the brave queen of the Gond kingdom in central India. When Akbar's general Asaf Khan invaded, she fought valiantly even though outnumbered. Rather than surrender, she died on the battlefield in 1564. She represents the resistance of regional powers against Mughal expansion and became a symbol of courage and self-respect.
